AlphaWrite Guide Toolkit

Motivation & Habits Playbook

The Guide Role Contract

Guides motivate students and enforce habits. You are not responsible for teaching writing skills—that's what the AlphaWrite curriculum does.

What Guides Do

  • Hold students accountable to the process
  • Build consistent habits around planning, revising, and using feedback
  • Keep students engaged and moving forward
  • Celebrate progress and effort
  • Redirect students to course materials when they need instruction

What Guides Do NOT Do

  • Teach sentence structure
  • Explain how to elaborate
  • Tell students what to write
  • Fix their writing for them
  • Diagnose grammar issues
  • Give content suggestions

Boundary Scripts

When students ask for writing help, use these redirects:

"How do I start this paragraph?" "Let's look at your outline. What does your topic sentence say?"
"Is this sentence good?" "Read it aloud. Does it sound complete to you?"
"What should I write next?" "Check your plan—what's your next point?"
"I don't understand elaboration." "The Student Toolkit has a section on that. Let's pull it up."
"Can you help me fix this?" "Use your revision checklist first, then show me what you changed."
When a student genuinely needs instruction: Route them to: the AlphaWrite course lesson, the Student Toolkit, or flag for escalation to an academic coach.

TWR Background

For Guide Context Only

This section helps you understand what students are learning. You will not teach this material—it's context so you can recognize when students are using (or skipping) their tools.

The TWR System in Brief

The Writing Revolution method teaches writing through:

  1. Sentence-level skills — Building strong, complete sentences
  2. Planning tools — SPOs (Single Paragraph Outlines) and MPOs (Multiple Paragraph Outlines)
  3. Structured revision — Systematic editing using checklists
  4. Incremental practice — Short, frequent writing beats long, rare assignments

Why Planning Matters

Students who skip planning: get stuck staring at blank screens, write scattered paragraphs, take longer to finish, and produce weaker work.

Your role: Enforce that planning happens before drafting. Don't teach how to plan—just require that the plan exists.

Why Revision Matters

Students who skip revision: lose points to careless errors, miss opportunities to strengthen ideas, and never internalize good habits.

Your role: Enforce that revision happens before submission. Don't teach what to revise—just require that revision occurs.

Behavior Patterns You Can Influence

These are the common ways students struggle. Your role is to influence the behavior, not fix the writing.

Pattern 1: Skipping Planning

What You'll See
  • Student jumps straight to drafting
  • "I don't need an outline"
  • Disorganized paragraphs, scattered ideas
What You Can Do
  • Require the plan before they draft: "Show me your completed SPO."
  • Make it a gate: "I see you're ready to write. Can I see your outline first?"
  • Reframe planning as a shortcut: "Writers who plan finish faster."
What You Cannot Do
  • Fill out the outline for them
  • Tell them what their supporting details should be
  • Explain how to write a topic sentence

Pattern 2: Rushing / Not Revising

What You'll See
  • Submits immediately after drafting
  • Careless errors (spelling, missing words, capitalization)
  • "I'm done!" (too quickly)
What You Can Do
  • Enforce a pause: "Before you submit, do a slow reread from the top."
  • Require checklist use: "Walk through CUPS, then show me you're ready."
  • Build in a speed bump: "Take 30 seconds. Find one thing to fix."
What You Cannot Do
  • Point out specific errors
  • Edit their work
  • Tell them what word is misspelled

Pattern 3: Ignoring Feedback

What You'll See
  • Same errors repeated across assignments
  • Doesn't implement suggestions
  • Treats feedback as "done" once read
What You Can Do
  • Require action: "What's one change you'll make based on this feedback?"
  • Require logging: "Add this to your Common Errors list."
  • Check follow-through: "Last time you got feedback on X. Show me how you addressed it this time."
What You Cannot Do
  • Explain what the feedback means
  • Show them how to fix it
  • Rewrite the sentence for them

Pattern 4: Stopping Too Soon (Thin Paragraphs)

What You'll See
  • Paragraphs with only 1-2 sentences
  • Supporting details without explanation
  • "I said everything I wanted to say"
What You Can Do
  • Prompt extension: "Take your idea one step further before you're done."
  • Point to the plan: "Your outline shows three details. I see one. What about the others?"
  • Use the Student Toolkit: "Check the 'So What?' section in your toolkit."
What You Cannot Do
  • Tell them what to add
  • Explain elaboration techniques
  • Suggest specific examples or reasons

Habit Routines

The Core Routine: Plan → Draft → Revise → Reflect

Every writing session should follow this sequence. Your job is to keep students on the routine.

PLAN
"Is your outline complete? Show me."
DRAFT
"Write from your plan. I'm here if you get stuck."
REVISE
"Before you submit: slow reread, then CUPS checklist."
REFLECT
"What's one thing you did well? One thing to work on next time?"

Session Structure

WARM-UP (2-3 min)
Mindset reset: "What's your focus today?"
Review: "Any feedback from last time to address?"
GOAL SETTING (1-2 min)
Choose one habit focus:
□ Complete planning before drafting
□ Use revision checklist before submitting
□ Apply one piece of prior feedback
□ Slow down and reread
WORK TIME (15-25 min)
Student works; guide monitors the routine
Prompt compliance, not content
CLOSE (2-3 min)
Reflect: "Did you hit your habit goal?"
Set next goal: "What will you focus on next session?"

Motivation Prompts by Phase

Before Writing

Instead of... Say...
"What's your topic sentence?" "Is your plan complete?"
"What are your three reasons?" "Show me your outline before you start."
"Let me help you brainstorm" "Use your planning tool first, then we'll check in."

During Writing

Instead of... Say...
"Add a 'because' here" "Check your plan—are you on track?"
"What example could you use?" "Your toolkit has prompts if you're stuck."
"This needs more detail" "Take your idea one step further."

After Writing (Revision)

Instead of... Say...
"Circle your topic sentence" "Walk through your checklist."
"You're missing elaboration here" "Do a slow reread from the top."
"This word is spelled wrong" "Run through CUPS before you submit."

After Feedback

Instead of... Say...
"This feedback means you should..." "What's one change you'll make?"
"Here's how to fix that" "Where in your writing will you make a change?"
"Let me show you" "Log this in your Common Errors list."

Motivational Strategies

For Reluctant Writers

  • Connect to their interests (AlphaWrite's strength)
  • Start with the smallest step: "Just get your outline done first."
  • Acknowledge the feeling: "I know writing feels hard. Let's just focus on the plan."
  • Celebrate completion of process steps, not just quality

For Perfectionists

  • Emphasize that first drafts are supposed to be rough
  • Time-box drafting: "Write for 10 minutes, then we'll look at revision."
  • Separate drafting from editing: "Right now, just get ideas down."

For "I'm Bad at Writing" Mindset

  • Point to process, not talent: "Writing is a skill. Skills improve with practice."
  • Highlight specific progress: "Last week, you skipped the outline. This week, you used it."
  • Normalize struggle: "Every writer finds this hard. You're doing the work."

For "I'm Done" (Too Fast)

  • Build in the speed bump: "Before you submit, 30 seconds of slow reading."
  • Make revision part of "done": "You're not done until you've run through CUPS."
  • Reframe: "The revision step is where good becomes great."

Escalation Protocol

When to Escalate

Route students to additional support when:

  • They consistently cannot complete plans despite using the tools
  • They don't understand feedback even after consulting their toolkit
  • They show persistent skill gaps that require instruction
  • They're significantly behind and need targeted intervention

How to Escalate

  1. Document the pattern: "Student has struggled with X for Y sessions"
  2. Note what you've tried: "Enforced planning routine, directed to toolkit"
  3. Flag for academic coach review
  4. Continue motivation support while escalation is processed

What Escalation Is NOT

  • A guide giving up
  • A sign of failure
  • Something to avoid

Escalation is appropriate routing. Guides are not instructors. When instruction is needed, route to instruction.

Quick Reference

"Enforce the routine. Require the tools. Redirect to resources. Celebrate the effort."

Five Things to Say Every Session

  1. "Show me your plan before you draft."
  2. "Do a slow reread before you submit."
  3. "What's one change you'll make from feedback?"
  4. "Take your idea one step further."
  5. "What will you focus on next time?"

Five Things to NEVER Say

  1. "Add a [specific sentence element] here."
  2. "Your topic sentence should be..."
  3. "This is how you elaborate..."
  4. "Let me show you how to fix this."
  5. "Here's what you should write..."

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"If I don't help them write, I'm not helping" Enforcing habits IS helping—it builds independence
"They need me to explain this concept" The curriculum and toolkit explain concepts; you enforce use
"It's faster if I just tell them" Short-term faster, long-term harmful—they don't learn the habit
"They're stuck, so I should give them ideas" Being stuck is part of learning; redirect to their plan and tools
"Motivation isn't enough" Motivation + habits + good curriculum = student success
