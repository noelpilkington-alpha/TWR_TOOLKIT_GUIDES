Part 1: The Writer's Mindset

1. Planning is not extra work—it's a shortcut

Writers who plan finish faster, write better, and don't get stuck. Skipping the plan means staring at a blank screen. The plan tells you exactly what to write.

2. First drafts are supposed to be messy

No one writes perfectly the first time. The goal of a first draft is to get ideas down. Revision is where good writing becomes great.

3. Feedback is fuel, not criticism

Feedback shows you exactly what to improve. If you apply it, you get better. If you ignore it, you make the same mistakes.

Part 2: Planning Tools

Minimum Viable Plan (Quick Version)

When you're short on time or the assignment is simple:

TOPIC SENTENCE: What's the main point?
→

SUPPORT 1: First reason/evidence?
→

SUPPORT 2: Second reason/evidence?
→

SO WHAT? Why does this matter?
→

If you get stuck while writing, upgrade to the full SPO.

Full SPO (Single Paragraph Outline)

Use this for any paragraph that matters:

TOPIC SENTENCE: What is the main point of this paragraph?
→

SUPPORTING DETAIL #1: What's one piece of evidence or reason?
→
ELABORATION: Why does this matter? So what?
→

SUPPORTING DETAIL #2: What's another piece of evidence?
→
ELABORATION: Why does this matter? So what?
→

SUPPORTING DETAIL #3: What's a third piece of evidence?
→
ELABORATION: Why does this matter? So what?
→

CONCLUDING SENTENCE: How do you wrap up this paragraph?
→

Part 3: When You're Stuck

The Because-But-So Technique

These three words unlock ideas when you don't know what to write:

Word What it does Example
BECAUSE Explains why "Video games can be educational because they develop problem-solving skills."
BUT Shows the other side "Video games can be educational, but excessive playing can interfere with other activities."
SO Shows the result "Video games develop problem-solving skills, so they may improve performance in math and science."

Try this: Take any sentence you've written. Add "because," "but," or "so" to make it stronger.

Sentence Starters

When you don't know how to begin, grab one:

To give an example:
  • For example, ...
  • One illustration of this is ...
  • This can be seen when ...
To explain further:
  • This means that ...
  • In other words, ...
  • This is important because ...
To show contrast:
  • However, ...
  • On the other hand, ...
  • Although some might argue ...
To conclude:
  • Therefore, ...
  • As a result, ...
  • In summary, ...

Part 4: Elaboration—The "So What?" Skill

The Problem

Most students write supporting details but don't explain them.

"Dogs are loyal."
(OK, but so what?)
"Dogs are loyal. This means they provide consistent emotional support, which can help people who live alone feel less isolated."
(Now I understand why it matters.)

Three Ways to Elaborate

After each supporting detail, ask yourself one of these questions:

Question What it does Example Starter
"So what?" Shows significance "This matters because..."
"Because..." Gives the reason "This happens because..."
"This shows that..." Interprets the evidence "This shows that..."

Practice: Take any fact or detail. Answer "So what?" in one sentence. That's elaboration.

Part 5: Paragraph Structure

Every Paragraph Needs 3 Things

  1. Topic Sentence — First sentence. States the main point.
  2. Supporting Details + Elaboration — Evidence + explanation of why it matters.
  3. Concluding Sentence — Wraps up the paragraph (not just "The End").

Paragraph Self-Check

Before you call a paragraph done:

  • Does my first sentence clearly state the main point?
  • Do I have at least 2-3 supporting details?
  • Did I explain WHY each detail matters (elaboration)?
  • Does my last sentence wrap up the paragraph?

Part 6: Revision

The Speed-Bump Ritual

Do this EVERY time before you submit.

1
PAUSE
Stop. Take 10 seconds. Step away mentally.
2
SLOW REREAD
Read from the top. Slower than feels natural.
3
FIX ONE THING
Find ONE obvious problem. There's always one.
4
CUPS CHECK
Caps, Usage, Punctuation, Spelling

ARMS (Big Picture Revision)

Use this when you have more time:

Letter Question Action
A What's missing? ADD more detail
R What's off-topic or repeated? REMOVE it
M Is everything in the right order? MOVE things
S Where can I use better words? SUBSTITUTE

The Read-Aloud Trick

Read your writing out loud.

When you read silently, your brain auto-corrects errors. When you read aloud, you hear:

  • Awkward sentences
  • Missing words
  • Run-ons
  • Spots that need more explanation

If you can't read aloud, whisper it or mouth the words.

Part 7: Using Feedback

The 20-Second Feedback Routine

When you get feedback, don't just read it—use it.

1
IDENTIFY (5 seconds)
What is the feedback asking me to change? Write it in ONE sentence.
2
LOCATE (5 seconds)
Where in my writing will I make the change? Point to the exact spot.
3
CHANGE (5 seconds)
Make ONE change right now.
4
LOG (5 seconds)

Part 8: Essay Structure

The Basic Essay Blueprint

INTRODUCTION
├── Hook: Grab attention (question, fact, scenario)
├── Context: Background the reader needs
└── Thesis: Your main argument + 2-3 reasons
BODY PARAGRAPH 1
├── Topic sentence (first reason from thesis)
├── Supporting details + elaboration
└── Transition to next paragraph
BODY PARAGRAPH 2
├── Topic sentence (second reason)
├── Supporting details + elaboration
└── Transition to next paragraph
BODY PARAGRAPH 3
├── Topic sentence (third reason)
├── Supporting details + elaboration
└── Lead into conclusion
CONCLUSION
├── Restate thesis (different words)
├── Summarize main points briefly
└── Final thought: Why this matters

The Thesis Formula

Strong thesis = Your position + Your reasons

"Video games are interesting."
(No clear position, no reasons)
"Video games should be used in education because they increase engagement, develop problem-solving skills, and teach collaboration."
(Clear position + three reasons that become body paragraphs)

Part 10: Growth Tracking

My Writing Goals

Remember

Writing is hard for everyone—including professional writers.

The difference between struggling writers and strong writers isn't talent. It's:

  1. Using the strategies — planning, structure, elaboration
  2. Revising seriously — not rushing, using the checklist
  3. Learning from feedback — applying it, logging it, not repeating errors

You have the tools. Use them.

Every piece of writing is a chance to get better. Take it.

Keep this toolkit open while you write. Refer to it before, during, and after every assignment.
